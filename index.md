    <h2>Project Overview</h2>
    <div class="overview-grid" style="display: grid; grid-template-columns: 1fr 1fr; gap: 2rem; margin-bottom: 2rem; align-items: center;">
        <div>
            <p>This website examines the environmental and social histories of Chinese immigrants in California from the mid-19th century to the present, focusing especially on how Chinese communities shaped and were shaped by land, water, labor, and environmental regulation. Using a Bay Area lens centered on Angel Island, San Francisco and Oakland Chinatowns, the Sacramento‚ÄìSan Joaquin Delta levees, and Sierra Nevada railroad corridors, the site explores:</p>

        </div>
    </div>
    
    <ul class="key-themes">
        <li>How U.S. federal and California state laws (e.g., Page Act, Chinese Exclusion Act, Alien Land Laws) structured access to land, property, water, and livelihoods.</li>
        <li>How Chinese labor transformed ecologies through mining, railroad construction, Delta levee building, agriculture, fisheries, and small-business work.</li>
        <li>How Chinatowns navigated environmental health risks, urban renewal, freeway construction, and redevelopment pressures.</li>
        <li>How post-1965 immigration reshaped environmental stewardship, greenspace, and community policy engagement.</li>
    </ul>
