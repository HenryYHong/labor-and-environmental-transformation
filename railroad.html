Mountain Infrastructure

Railroad Corridors & Ecologies

Chinese railroad workers carved the Sierra Nevada, permanently altering mountain landscapes.

The Central Pacific Railroad relied on thousands of Chinese laborers who blasted tunnels, laid track, and built infrastructure through extreme terrain. Their efforts connected the continent while transforming mountain ecologies.

Building the Transcontinental Railroad

Chinese workers made up approximately 80% of the Central Pacific Railroad workforce, performing the most dangerous and technically challenging work. They labored year-round in snow, freezing temperatures, and dangerous blasting zones, often suspended from cliffs in baskets to drill charges into solid granite. The work required extraordinary skill, courage, and endurance.

Workforce Composition and Organization

Chinese workers were organized into work gangs led by Chinese labor contractors who spoke both English and Chinese dialects. These contractors recruited workers, negotiated wages, and managed daily operations. The workforce included not only laborers but also skilled workers including blacksmiths, carpenters, cooks, and engineers.

Initially, Central Pacific executives were skeptical about hiring Chinese workers, but after white workers proved unreliable and expensive, they turned to Chinese labor. Chinese workers quickly proved their value, working more efficiently and reliably than their white counterparts. However, they were paid less and assigned the most dangerous tasks.

Environmental Impacts

The railroad construction had profound and lasting environmental impacts:

  • Tunnel Blasting: Nitroglycerin opened 15 tunnels through solid granite, including the 1,659-foot Summit Tunnel at Donner Pass. The blasting permanently altered mountain geology, redirected groundwater, and created new erosion patterns.
  • Grade Construction: Cut-and-fill operations reshaped mountain slopes, moving millions of cubic yards of earth and rock. This work affected erosion, drainage, vegetation, and wildlife habitat.
  • Snow Sheds: Miles of timber sheds protected tracks from avalanches but also altered microclimates, affected wildlife movement, and required extensive logging.
  • Retaining Walls: Massive stone retaining walls were built to support grades, creating new geological features and affecting water flow.

Tunnel Blasting in Detail

The Summit Tunnel at Donner Pass required workers to drill through 1,659 feet of solid granite. Workers used hand drills and black powder initially, but later switched to nitroglycerin for faster progress. The work was extremely dangerous—workers drilled holes, inserted explosives, and then quickly retreated before detonation. Accidents were common, and many workers died from explosions, rock falls, and tunnel collapses.

Workers had to work from both ends of the tunnel and meet in the middle, a feat requiring extraordinary precision. The tunnel work required 24-hour shifts, with workers laboring in darkness, dust, and danger. The blasting operations permanently altered the mountain's geology, creating new passages that changed water flow patterns and affected local ecosystems.

Workplace Hazards

Chinese railroad workers faced extreme dangers throughout construction. Avalanches killed dozens of workers during winter construction in the high Sierra. Explosions from nitroglycerin accidents killed and injured many more. Falls from cliffs, trestles, and construction sites were common. Exposure to harsh mountain weather, including deep snow and freezing temperatures, caused illness and death.

Specific Hazards

  • Avalanches: Workers were killed by avalanches while working in high mountain passes during winter. The risk was so great that snow sheds were eventually built to protect tracks, but construction workers had no such protection.
  • Explosions: Nitroglycerin was extremely unstable and dangerous. Accidents killed and injured many workers, with explosions sometimes destroying entire work camps.
  • Falls: Workers fell from cliffs, trestles, and construction sites, often with fatal consequences. The work required balancing on narrow ledges and working in baskets suspended from cliffs.
  • Exposure: Workers faced extreme weather conditions, including deep snow, freezing temperatures, and blizzards. Many suffered from frostbite, hypothermia, and other weather-related illnesses.
  • Disease: Crowded living conditions, poor sanitation, and exposure to harsh conditions led to outbreaks of disease including dysentery, pneumonia, and other illnesses.

The 1867 Strike

In June 1867, thousands of Chinese workers went on strike, demanding higher wages, shorter hours, and better working conditions. The strike demonstrated Chinese workers' capacity for collective action and challenged stereotypes about passive, docile laborers. The company broke the strike by cutting off food supplies, but the action showed that Chinese workers would organize to improve their conditions despite exclusion and discrimination.

The strike was significant because it demonstrated that Chinese workers were not passive victims but active agents who would fight for their rights. It also showed the company's dependence on Chinese labor—when the strike began, construction came to a halt, demonstrating the essential role of Chinese workers in the project.

Long-term Ecological Change

Railroad corridors enabled increased resource extraction, tourism, and development in the Sierra Nevada, multiplying the environmental impacts of the original construction. The railroad made previously inaccessible areas accessible, leading to logging, mining, and settlement that further transformed mountain ecosystems.

Resource Extraction

The railroad enabled increased logging, mining, and other resource extraction in the Sierra Nevada. Previously inaccessible forests could now be logged and transported to markets. Mining operations expanded, using the railroad to transport ore and equipment. These activities further transformed mountain ecosystems, creating cumulative environmental impacts.

Tourism and Development

The railroad also enabled tourism and development in the Sierra Nevada. Resorts, hotels, and recreational facilities were built along the route, bringing more people and development to previously remote areas. This development further altered ecosystems and increased environmental pressures.

Ongoing Environmental Challenges

Today, the Sierra Nevada faces ongoing environmental challenges that connect to the railroad's construction. Altered waterways, new settlement patterns, and increased wildfire risk continue to shape the region. Climate change is intensifying these challenges, with changing snow patterns, increased fire risk, and ecosystem shifts affecting the mountains that Chinese workers helped transform.

Memory and Recognition

For decades, Chinese contributions to the transcontinental railroad were largely omitted from official histories. The famous "Golden Spike" ceremony at Promontory Summit, Utah, in 1869 did not include any Chinese workers, despite their essential role in building the railroad. Historical accounts often focused on white engineers and executives while ignoring the Chinese workers who did the actual construction.

Recent Recognition

Recent scholarship, memorials, and public history projects have begun to recognize Chinese contributions. Books like Gordon Chang's "Ghosts of Gold Mountain" have brought attention to Chinese railroad workers. Memorials have been erected at various sites along the route, and museums have added exhibits documenting Chinese contributions.

However, much work remains to fully document and acknowledge the role of Chinese workers. Many workers died during construction and were buried in unmarked graves along the route. Their names and stories are largely lost to history, creating a challenge for memorialization and recognition.

Contemporary Relevance

Understanding the history of Chinese railroad workers is essential for addressing contemporary issues including labor rights, infrastructure development, and environmental justice. The railroad's construction demonstrates how infrastructure projects can transform environments while also raising questions about who benefits from these transformations and who bears the risks.
